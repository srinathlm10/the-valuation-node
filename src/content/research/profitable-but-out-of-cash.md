## The statement that confuses every beginner

A company reports a profit of 100 crore for the year. Surely it has 100 crore more in the bank than it started with? No, and the difference is not a technicality. It is one of the most important ideas in financial analysis.

Profit is calculated on the accrual basis: revenue is recorded when it is earned, not when the cash arrives, and expenses are recorded when they are incurred, not when they are paid. This is the right way to measure economic performance, but it means the income statement and the bank account can tell completely different stories at the same time. A company can book large profits while its cash drains away, and if the cash runs out, the profits on paper will not save it. Insolvency is a cash event, not a profit event.

There are three main mechanics that open this gap. Understanding them is the whole game.

## Mechanic one: sales that have not been paid for

When a company sells on credit, it records the revenue immediately, but the cash arrives only when the customer pays, which might be 30, 60, or 120 days later. The unpaid amount sits on the balance sheet as receivables.

Now imagine a company growing fast. Every month it sells more than the last, and every month the pile of unpaid invoices grows with it. The income statement shows expanding profits. The bank account shows money going out (to produce the goods) far ahead of money coming in (from customers who have not paid yet). The faster the growth, the wider the gap.

A simplified illustration:

| Item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 500 | 750 | 1,100 |
| Net profit | 50 | 75 | 110 |
| Receivables at year end | 80 | 160 | 300 |
| Cash from operations | 45 | 20 | (15) |

Profit more than doubles across three years. Cash from operations goes negative. The reason is visible in the receivables line: an ever-growing share of the "profit" exists only as promises from customers. The company is effectively lending its customers more money every year, and it must fund that lending from somewhere: its own cash, bank borrowing, or new equity. If none of those are available when needed, a profitable company misses payroll.

## Mechanic two: cash trapped in inventory

The second trap is stock. A company buys or manufactures inventory with real cash today, but that spending does not touch the income statement until the goods are sold, when it appears as cost of goods sold. Until then, the cash is gone and the inventory just sits on the balance sheet.

A company preparing for growth, or misjudging demand, can pour enormous cash into inventory while reporting healthy profits on the smaller amount it actually sold. If demand disappoints, the cash is locked in a warehouse, and discounting the stock to release the cash destroys next year's margins. Either way, the bank balance suffered long before the income statement admitted anything was wrong.

## Mechanic three: heavy investment ahead of returns

The third mechanic is capital expenditure. When a company builds a factory, the cash leaves immediately, but the income statement spreads the cost over many years as depreciation. A company in a heavy investment phase can therefore report solid profits (because only a fraction of the spending hits this year's expenses) while consuming far more cash than it generates.

This is not automatically bad. Investment ahead of returns is how companies grow. But it means the company is dependent on external funding until the investments start paying, and that dependence is invisible on the income statement. It shows up only in [cash flow](/learn/foundations/accounting/reading-a-cash-flow-statement).

## The formula that ties it together

The bridge between profit and operating cash is, in simplified form:

```
Cash from operations = Net profit + Depreciation
                       − Increase in receivables
                       − Increase in inventory
                       + Increase in payables
```

Each term tells you something. Depreciation is added back because it is an expense that costs no cash this year. Rising receivables and inventory subtract, because they are profit and spending that have not converted to cash. Rising payables add, because delaying payment to suppliers preserves cash (though it cannot be stretched forever).

The practical habit: whenever you see a profit figure, find cash from operations and compare. If profit consistently outruns operating cash, the gap is being parked in receivables and inventory, and someone has to finance that gap. Ask who, and for how long they will keep doing it.

## Why this kills companies in good times, not bad

The counterintuitive part: this failure mode is most dangerous during rapid growth. A shrinking company collects old receivables and sells down inventory, which releases cash even as profits fall. A booming company does the opposite, swallowing cash to fund ever-larger receivables and stock. This is why businesses fail during expansions more often than intuition suggests, a pattern known as overtrading: growing faster than your [working capital](/learn/foundations/corporate-finance/working-capital) can support.

The lesson is not that profit is meaningless. It is that profit and cash answer different questions. Profit asks whether the business model works economically. Cash asks whether the company survives long enough to prove it. [Free cash flow](/learn/foundations/valuation/dcf-theory-and-mechanics) sits at the centre of every serious valuation for exactly this reason.

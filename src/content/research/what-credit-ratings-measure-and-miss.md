## The one question a rating answers

A credit rating is an opinion about a single thing: the likelihood that a borrower will fail to pay its debt obligations in full and on time. That is the entire question. Not whether the company is well run, not whether its stock is attractive, not whether its products are good. Only: will lenders get their money back?

This narrowness explains outcomes that otherwise look strange. A slow, boring company with modest growth but fortress-like cash flows can carry a top rating. A brilliant, fast-growing innovator burning cash can sit far down the scale. The rating is not disagreeing with the market's enthusiasm; it is answering a different question. Equity holders are paid from upside. Lenders are paid from stability. Ratings measure stability. The Foundations topic on [credit risk fundamentals](/learn/foundations/credit-analysis/credit-risk-fundamentals) builds this lender's lens from scratch.

## What actually goes into one

Rating agencies assess broadly four layers, weighted by judgment rather than formula:

**Business risk:** how stable and defensible the cash flows are. Industry cyclicality, competitive position, diversification of customers and geographies. A commodity producer hostage to prices carries more business risk than a utility with regulated returns, at identical financial ratios.

**Financial risk:** the balance sheet arithmetic. Leverage (debt against earnings, commonly debt to EBITDA), coverage (earnings against interest, such as [interest cover](/learn/foundations/financial-statement-analysis/solvency-ratios)), and cash flow adequacy against upcoming repayments. These ratios anchor the rating, but thresholds shift with business risk: a stable business can safely carry leverage that would sink a cyclical one.

**Liquidity:** whether the borrower can meet obligations over the next twelve to eighteen months specifically, from cash, committed bank lines, and manageable maturities. Solvency is the long-run question; liquidity is whether you survive to reach the long run. Companies default from liquidity failures even while technically solvent.

**Support and structure:** whether anyone stands behind the borrower (a strong parent, a government) and where the specific instrument sits in the repayment queue. This is why one company's different bonds can carry different ratings.

The output is expressed on the familiar ladder (AAA down through AA, A, BBB, then the speculative grades BB, B, and below), where each step down represents meaningfully higher default probability, and the gap between steps widens sharply toward the bottom. [Reading the agencies' actual reports](/learn/foundations/credit-analysis/reading-crisil-icra-moodys-reports) is where the useful detail lives.

## What ratings systematically miss

Ratings have well-documented blind spots, and knowing them is what separates using ratings from outsourcing your thinking to them.

**They lag.** Agencies value rating stability and revise deliberately, usually after evidence accumulates. Markets reprice credit risk in days; ratings adjust in months. By the time a downgrade arrives, bond prices have typically moved long before. A rating tells you the assessed position, rarely the fresh news.

**They compress cliff risks.** A rating expresses a central expectation. It handles gradual deterioration well and sudden ruptures poorly: fraud, governance collapse, an overnight funding freeze. History's most painful defaults include borrowers rated investment grade shortly before failing, not because analysts were fools, but because discontinuities are precisely what a smooth scale cannot express.

**They depend on reported numbers.** A rating is built from the company's own statements. If the statements are wrong (aggressive recognition, hidden liabilities, related-party games), the rating inherits the fiction. Ratings verify consistency and probe management, but they are not forensic audits.

**They carry a structural conflict.** In the dominant model, the issuer pays the agency for its own rating. Agencies manage this with real safeguards, and reputational damage is genuinely costly to them, but the incentive tilt exists and has mattered historically, most visibly in structured finance before 2008.

**They say nothing about price.** A correctly rated bond can still be a terrible investment if it yields too little for its risk, and a low-rated bond can be a bargain at the right price. Ratings order borrowers by default likelihood; they do not tell you what compensation is adequate. That judgment, the [credit spread](/learn/glossary/credit-spread) question, is the investor's own.

## How to actually use a rating

Treat the rating as a structured starting point, then do three things it cannot do for you. Read the rating rationale rather than the letter grade, because the rationale names the specific strengths, risks, and triggers the agency is watching, which is the genuinely useful content. Watch the outlook and any watch listings, which telegraph likely direction. And check the recent-momentum signals ratings lag behind: bond spreads, refinancing terms, liquidity events, and the red flags in the financial statements themselves.

A rating is one informed opinion about one narrow question, produced under known constraints. Used that way, it is valuable. Used as a substitute for analysis, it is how surprises happen.

## Why red flags are about relationships, not numbers

No single number in a financial statement is a red flag by itself. Trouble shows up in relationships: two lines that should move together but do not, or a trend that only makes sense if something unstated is happening. Each flag below is a relationship to check, what it can mean, and the innocent explanation you must rule out before concluding anything. A red flag is a reason to investigate, not a verdict.

## Flag one: profit rising while operating cash flow stagnates

The most important single check in financial analysis. Net profit and [cash from operations](/learn/foundations/accounting/reading-a-cash-flow-statement) should broadly track each other over time. When profit climbs year after year but operating cash flow is flat or falling, the gap is accumulating somewhere on the balance sheet, usually in receivables or inventory.

| Item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Net profit | 100 | 130 | 165 |
| Cash from operations | 95 | 75 | 40 |

The pattern above says: reported earnings are increasingly made of paper, not cash. Possible causes range from aggressive revenue recognition (booking sales earlier than cash reality supports) to customers who cannot pay. The innocent explanation is a deliberate, temporary working capital build for growth. Three years of widening divergence makes the innocent explanation harder to sustain.

## Flag two: receivables growing faster than revenue

If sales grow 15 percent and receivables grow 40 percent, customers are taking longer to pay, or the company is booking sales its customers have not agreed to on normal terms. Watch the trend in receivable days (receivables divided by revenue, times 365). A steady climb means each rupee of reported revenue is taking longer to become real cash.

Why it matters: stretching credit terms is the easiest way to manufacture revenue growth. Offer customers 120 days to pay instead of 45, and sales jump today while the cash problem arrives quietly later. The innocent explanation is a changed customer mix (more institutional buyers with longer terms). Check whether management explains the change and whether the trend stabilizes.

## Flag three: frequent "one-time" items that keep recurring

Exceptional items are supposed to be exceptional. When restructuring charges, impairments, or "one-off" losses appear every single year, they are not one-offs; they are operating costs wearing a costume. The effect is to make "adjusted" or "core" profit look permanently better than the actual reported result. The Foundations topic on [common adjustments](/learn/foundations/accounting/common-adjustments) covers how to normalise for these properly.

The check: line up five years of exceptional items. If they land in the same direction (always reducing the profit that management asks you to ignore) and appear most years, treat the adjusted numbers with suspicion and anchor on the unadjusted ones. The innocent explanation is genuine transformation (a real restructuring has a start and an end). Recurring forever is the tell.

## Flag four: debt rising while the business supposedly thrives

A company reporting strong profits and healthy growth should, over time, need less external money, not more. When borrowings climb year after year alongside supposedly excellent results, ask what the debt is funding. Sometimes the answer is legitimate expansion. Sometimes the debt is quietly funding the gap between paper profits and cash reality (see flag one), or dividends the company cannot actually afford.

The check: compare the growth in borrowings against free cash flow over three to five years. A thriving business generating real cash pays debt down or holds it steady. Persistent borrowing against a backdrop of reported success means the success is not producing cash, and the statements are telling two contradictory stories. One of them is wrong.

## Flag five: auditor changes, resignations, or qualified opinions

The statements themselves can warn you through their gatekeepers. An auditor resigning mid-term, repeated changes of audit firm, a qualified opinion, or growing "emphasis of matter" paragraphs are among the strongest signals available, because auditors see far more than the published statements show and rarely walk away from fee-paying clients without reason.

Related governance signals worth the same weight: senior finance leadership departing abruptly, delayed filing of results, and pledged promoter shareholdings rising. None of these is proof of anything. All of them are reasons to slow down and dig before trusting the numbers.

## Using the checklist properly

Two habits make these flags useful rather than paranoid. First, always check across multiple years; nearly every flag above is a trend, invisible in a single statement. Second, always seek the innocent explanation and test it, because real analysis is distinguishing the benign case from the dangerous one, not pattern-matching to accusations. A company with one flag deserves questions. A company with three or four deserves your absence.
